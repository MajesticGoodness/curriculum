Intro

"A sufficiently expressive programming language is the foundations for all of mathematics" - Robert Harper OPLSS 2018

The goal is to understand the recent developments in computational type theory which came from Per Marin-Lof's intuitionistic type theory, and Vladimir Voevodsky's univalent foundations. Voedvodsky's plan was to create a new foundation for 'all of mathematics', create new proof assistants so mathematicians without pedigree can contribute to global mathematics research since their proofs can easily be verified, that AI can extend a model (itself) and generate it's own proofs for that model, and that math will in the future be done entirely in a programming language because the theory of programming languages he claims is the same thing as mathematical logic, they express the same ideas in a different language, so why not unify them.

Voevodsky

You should google and read everything you can about Field's medalist Voevodsky and his work at the Institute of Advanced Studies at Princeton. He has many talks on YouTube as well well worth viewing, too many to list here but we'll watch some later in the workshop.

Here is the quick rundown: Voevodsky found modern math was too complicated for anybody including his peers at the IAS to trust outsider research because there were too many problems found in old proofs including problems found in his own proofs. Unless you had some prestigious university pedigree nobody would be able to trust your proofs, so nobody even looks at them. In other words modern math is completely broken as a field and the best way to fix this he thought, was computer assisted proofs that way if some non-prestigious pedigree mathematician uploaded a paper it would come with a proof as a program you could run and verify it first so you knew you weren't wasting your time.

Our existing proof assistants then were trash, and unable to be verified by humans printing hundreds of pages of first-order logic so long story short, an enormous amount of mathematicians and type theory specialists were assembled to produce Homotopy Type Theory (see the HoTT book). This proved to be a dead end (not entirely, as we'll see later) and termed a 'misadventure in formalisms' by Robert Harper who deemed it insufficiently computational, and then these ideas became Computational Type Theory which is what we are taking here.

There is a very good interview with Voevodsky here:

When I first started to explore the possibility, computer proof verification was almost a forbidden subject among mathematicians. A conversation about the need for computer proof assistants would invariably drift to Gödel’s incompleteness theorem (which has nothing to do with the actual problem) or to one or two cases of verification of already existing proofs, which were used only to demonstrate how impractical the whole idea was.

Voevodsky spent a year wandering around hallucinating (he died of a brain aneurysm) and recorded this experience before he claims he eliminated the hallucinations by focusing on work and returning to IAS where he invented the univalent foundations. There are interesting interviews you have to google translate here and here it's refreshing to see an expert in one field with the highest honors you can receive (Field's medal) be able to talk freely about other subjects in the sciences or talk about totally crazy things they themselves have put in time to understand.

Regardless what you think of Voevodsky his conjecture that modern math is in 'a crisis' is probably true, if you come from nowhere university and write up a proof of some obscure abstract lattice nobody else is going to bother to verify it or even read it. Voevodsky tried to fix this and his efforts continue long after his death.

Per Martin-Lof

A Swedish logician who is the best writer you'll ever read, and one of the most polished and amazing lecturers you'll ever hear he takes very advanced subjects and breaks them down that anybody at any level can understand. We will read all of his papers here which Robert Harper recommends if you want to understand constructive type theory, and hear some of his talks about logic/judgements.

Robert Harper

Most of our material will consist of his lectures and draft book on Computational Type Theory, his Standard ML book, and his book PFPL. He also has a Homotopy type theory recorded lecture seminar we may do time allowing because it's great for explaining higher dimensional types.

Robert Harper purposely makes his lectures provocative in order to elicit a response in the reviewer to investigate themselves what he's talking about it which is always a good strategy, so you will be interested in his lectures. He will hand out very blunt and honest answers, again this will be very refreshing as a viewer/learner/casual to witness.

His bluntness has rubbed off on his PhD students, so we will also take courses and papers from Dan Licata, Carlo Angiuli and Jon Sterling who is also not scared to write opinions. I am primarily interested in opinionated researchers even if I totally disagree politically just to break out of the coma that is modern orthodox academia. If they are opinionated they are passionate about whatever it is they're doing and it's probably worth out time to follow their research. For example Robert Harper's book PFPL is the only PL theory book I know of where the professor who wrote it insists 'this is how you do it and everything else is wrong'. Normally you are given a vague overview and not concrete instructions how to write a language spec.
